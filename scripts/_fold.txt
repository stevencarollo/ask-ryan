Attorney's-eye view: organize the title strategy BEFORE court. 'I don't call them problems - I call them challenges.'
RESEARCHED (Flip Talk interview transcript; Real Estate Excellence writeup; alnicoletti.com; The Al Nicoletti Show). Florida probate attorney ('Prince of Probate'); practice = probate, quiet title, partition, trust administration; fees can wait for the TITLE CLOSING (agent/investor-friendly). Signature system: organize ALL documentation and the title strategy BEFORE court involvement - straightforward probates run days-to-4-weeks; he closed a TEN-HEIR probate in EIGHT DAYS. THREE-PLAYER RULE: court approval alone closes nothing - 'we've got all these other players - title, underwriters, judges. You need someone who knows all three'; underwriters can reject what a judge approved. Deal assessment sequence: (1) title company FIRST ('What do you need from me to resolve this? What are my options?'), (2) specialist attorney forced to answer 'How long and how much?', (3) exit-strategy analysis, (4) vet seller commitment. Quality doctrine: 'Focus on the quality of the lead over the quantity' - one cooperative $100K-spread deal beats chaos. Case proof: six probates on one title (8 owners, 6 deceased, genealogy traced to the 1950s, tax-deed auction looming, prior attorneys failed) - all resolved; investor in $15-20K, out ~$200K. Unsticks: fraud deeds, ancient unreconveyed mortgages, missing/international heirs (embassy paperwork), wetlands layered on probate. Mistakes he calls out: paying a generalist to LEARN your problem ('when lawyers do everything, it gets crazy... they may not even follow up'), ignoring exit strategy, assuming court approval = closing, uncommitted sellers. Specialization creed: niche = gravitation + credibility. Reframe everything: 'I don't call them problems anymore. I call them challenges.'
