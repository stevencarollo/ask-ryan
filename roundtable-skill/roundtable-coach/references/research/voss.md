# Chris Voss — Negotiation Psychology (Researched Dossier)

## Credentials
- Former FBI lead international kidnapping negotiator; author of "Never Split the Difference" — the most-cited negotiation book in real estate training; founder of The Black Swan Group.

## Core Tactics (verified mechanics)
- **Mirroring**: repeat the last 1-3 words as a question, then silence. Inner intention: "Please, help me understand."
- **Labeling**: "It seems like... / It sounds like..." (NEVER "I'm hearing" — puts people on guard). Follow with SILENCE — let the label work. Validates without conceding.
- **Accusation Audit**: list every negative they could think about you and say it FIRST ("taking the sting out") — spoken aloud, exaggerated accusations lose credibility.
- **Calibrated questions**: open-ended how/what that make THEM solve your problem — "How am I supposed to do that?", "What about this is important to you?", "How will we know we're on track?" Avoid can/is/are/do/does questions.
- **"That's right" vs "you're right"**: summarize their world until they say "THAT'S right" (breakthrough); "you're right" is a polite brush-off.
- **No-oriented questions**: "no" makes people feel safe and in control — negotiation BEGINS at no. "Is now a bad time to talk?" beats "Do you have a minute?" Reframe: no often means "I'm not ready / I don't understand / I need more information."
- **The Rule of Three**: get agreement to the same thing three ways in one conversation — initial yes, a summary that triggers "that's right," and a calibrated implementation question.

## The Ackerman Bargaining Model (exact sequence)
- Set your target → open at **65%** of it → raise in decreasing increments to **85% → 95% → 100%**. Use precise NON-ROUND numbers ($37,263, not $37,000 — reads as calculated) and attach a small non-cash item at the end to signal you're at your limit. Let the other side anchor first (usually).

## Communication Craft
- **Late-night FM DJ voice**: deep, soft, slow, reassuring — for tension moments; default to positive/playful warmth otherwise.
- **7-38-55**: when tone/body contradicts words, LABEL the incongruence ("It seems like something about this doesn't sit right...").
- **Pronoun analysis**: liars use more third-person pronouns and complex sentences; real decision-makers use FEWER "I" statements (the "I"-heavy talker is rarely the decider).
- **The Fair technique**: deploy "fair" early — "I want you to feel like you are being treated fairly at all times." If accused of unfairness: "Okay, I apologize. Let's stop and go back to where I started treating you unfairly and we'll fix it."

## Bending Reality
- Loss aversion beats gain framing — show what they LOSE without the deal. Anchor emotions first (accusation audit), then numbers.

## Black Swans
- Every negotiation hides 3+ unknown unknowns that change everything. Hunt them: dig into the other side's "religion" (worldview, constraints, hidden pressures), review everything with teammates, seek unguarded face time at the start/end of sessions, use their norms as leverage.

## Principles
- Slow it down; information-gathering beats early agreement; never split the difference — TRADE instead; "No deal is better than a bad deal"; a yes without an implementation "how" is meaningless.

## Real Estate Application (per RE-focused coverage)
- Sequence for any hard conversation: mirror (draw them out) → label (name the emotion) → calibrated question (hand them the problem). Works on lowball buyers, unrealistic sellers, and cross-agents identically.
