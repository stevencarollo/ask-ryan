# Ted Thomas — Tax Liens & Tax-Defaulted Property (Researched Dossier)

## Philosophy
- 30+ years as "America's Tax Lien Certificate & Tax Deed Authority." Chose the niche because it is "certain, predictable, and secure" — you invest directly with the GOVERNMENT, protected by the property tax code, not with a broker.
- "Education is important. If you don't get educated, you're doomed." Start part-time, learn systematically, use experienced coaching; the first deal is the hardest.
- Cardinal rule: "I won't buy anything unless I look at it or I have someone look at it."

## Core Mechanics (his teaching)
- **Tax LIEN certificates**: "a tax lien is nothing more than a piece of paper" — you buy the delinquent tax debt, the county pays you back principal + statutory interest when the owner redeems. About half the states sell liens. Passive; no possession. ~97% of certificates pay off ("successful investors with 15+ years report never having one that didn't"). If unredeemed → you can foreclose and receive the property, typically free of the mortgage.
- **Tax DEEDS**: the county confiscates and auctions the actual title for back taxes — "mortgage-free real estate for 10, 20 or 30 cents on the dollar." All states have some deed process; California is a deed state (5+ years delinquent → county auction).
- **State interest rates he cites**: Arizona 16%, Florida 18%, Illinois 18%, Iowa 24%, Texas up to 25% (redemption penalty); national range ~8-50%. Every state's rules differ — know YOUR county's code.

## Named Frameworks
- **The 20-30-3-1 auction funnel**: research 20-30 properties, expect to bid on ~3, win 1-2. Set the maximum bid from exit math BEFORE arriving; "be prepared to walk away."
- **Due-diligence stack**: county list/brochure → assessed value vs starting bid → GIS/satellite screening → PHYSICAL drive-by (or hire eyes) → neighborhood check ("you want a decent house in that neighborhood... you don't want the best house in the ghetto") → comps for true market value → verify surviving liens (IRS/municipal windows) → exit strategy documented before bidding. "Don't rely solely on the county's assessment."
- **Bid-thinning insight**: competition collapses as price rises — at $50K half the room exits; at $100K, 90%; at $125K, 95%. Higher-value targets = fewer bidders. In-room auctions beat online ("in the room, people push the price down; the buyers of the future are the people that buy online" — online bidding inflates prices).
- **Payment mechanics**: cashier's checks, never cash; deposit rules cap bidding power (e.g., Broward County FL: max bid = 20x your deposit); payment deadlines range from immediate to 15+ days by county.
- **Post-auction sequence**: (1) SECURE the property immediately; (2) quiet-title action via experienced attorney ($2,000-$5,000, 2-18 months) before resale with title insurance — EXCEPTION: seller-financing exit skips quiet title since you keep title; (3) exit.
- **Exit strategies ranked**: retail sale; wholesale to rehabbers; SELLER FINANCING at 8-11% for sub-$200K homes to credit-rejected buyers — "People will come and pay you MORE than you're asking if you'll finance it"; installment sales.

## Common Mistakes He Calls Out
1. Buying without looking (lazy selection).
2. Cheap houses in dying neighborhoods ("penny deals" under 20% of value are usually penny properties).
3. Undercapitalization; 4. no education; 5. ignoring repair-cost margins; 6. not knowing the county's specific rules (deposits, payment windows, redemption).
- The two-bucket summary he endorses: all failures = "not knowing the rules" + "not doing your homework."

## Agent Angle (for The Roundtable's use)

- Excess proceeds: auction overbids above taxes owed belong to the FORMER owner — a legitimate service niche helping them claim it.
